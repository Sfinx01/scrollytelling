<ScrollySection id="promise">
  <div class="section-label">Section 01</div>
  ## The Promise of Additionality
  
  Green bonds are portrayed as financial instruments that facilitate capital for new environmental projects. 
  
  The core driver behind these instruments is "additionality." This means mobilizing private sector investment that would not have occurred otherwise. If a green bond simply funds a project that would have been financed anyway through conventional debt, no additional environmental value is created.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="flaw">
  <div class="section-label">Section 02</div>
  ## The Structural Flaw
  
  Unlike sustainability-linked bonds, standard green bonds lack an enforceable "green default." If an issuer fails to meet environmental commitments, they cannot be sued by bondholders.
  
  Because capital is fungible and green bonds are backed by the issuer's overall cash flow, the link between the raised funds and the green projects is symbolic rather than strictly financial. This creates a system where firms can raise green capital while using it to replace conventional investments, leaving their total environmental output unchanged.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="methodology">
  <div class="section-label">Section 03</div>
  ## The Litmus Test
  
  To test whether corporate green bond issuance produces measurable real-economy effects, this research directly examined firm-level capital expenditure and carbon intensity.
  
  Using a stacked difference-in-differences approach, 179 green bond issuers were paired with 294 observationally similar conventional bond issuers. They were strictly matched based on firm size, region, industry, and pre-issuance emissions trends to ensure a valid baseline comparison.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="capex">
  <div class="section-label">Finding 01</div>
  ## The Investment Reality
  
  If green bonds genuinely mobilize new capital for green projects, we should observe an increase in capital expenditure compared to conventional issuers.
  
  The results indicate a tight null. Green bond issuance shows no measurable effect on capital expenditure. The difference between the treated and control groups was near zero, effectively ruling out strong investment responses to green bond issuance. 
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="emissions">
  <div class="section-label">Finding 02</div>
  ## Inconclusive Emissions
  
  The second measure of physical transition is a reduction in Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas intensity. 
  
  The results here were highly imprecise. The data exhibited high residual variance, meaning the confidence intervals included both large emissions reductions and large emissions increases. Consequently, the carbon intensity result cannot support meaningful conclusions about the environmental impact of these bonds.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="conclusion">
  <div class="section-label">Section 06</div>
  ## Signaling, Not Transition
  
  These findings do not necessarily imply greenwashing, as issuers are not breaching their reporting rules or agreements. 
  
  Instead, the data suggests that green bonds primarily function as a signaling tool. They help capital markets identify firms that are already green, rather than acting as a catalyst to change firm behaviour or increase sustainable investment levels. Any unintended consequences are outcomes of the instrument's fundamental design.
</ScrollySection>
