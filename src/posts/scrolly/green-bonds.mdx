<ScrollySection id="promise">
  <div class="section-label">Section 01</div>
  ## The Promise of Additionality

  Green bonds are sold on a simple promise: that labelling debt as green channels capital toward environmental projects that would not otherwise have been financed. This is additionality, the entire reason a separate climate-finance instrument is supposed to exist.

  The market has grown explosively on the assumption that issuance drives real physical transition. Yet almost no study has tested the first link in that chain directly: does issuing a green bond actually raise what a firm invests?
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="machine">
  <div class="section-label">Section 02</div>
  ## The Chain Everyone Assumes

  The assumed mechanism runs in a straight line. Issuance raises capital expenditure (H1), that investment lowers Scope 1 and 2 carbon intensity (H2), and an externally reviewed label should strengthen the first link (H3), since certification is a costlier and harder-to-fake signal of commitment (Flammer, 2021).

  This is the difference between stated and revealed additionality. Stated additionality is what issuers claim through the label and use-of-proceeds reporting. Revealed additionality is what issuers do with their balance sheet. The test is whether the first turns into the second.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="methodology">
  <div class="section-label">Section 03</div>
  ## A Direct Test

  A clean test needs near-twins. From a master panel of 21,510 firms, 743 green issuers matched to Compustat fall to 439 once financials are removed and 333 within the 2015 to 2022 cohorts; cohort-aware Coarsened Exact Matching then pairs 179 green issuers with 294 conventional issuers on firm size, region, industry and pre-issuance emissions trend (Iacus, King and Porro, 2012). The panel integrates LSEG, S&P Trucost, Compustat and Mergent FISD.

  The chart is the matching diagnostic. Before matching, treated and control firms differ on the covariates that drive investment; after matching, every standardised difference collapses toward zero. The matched panel is then estimated with stacked difference-in-differences and cross-checked with the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator, which stays unbiased under staggered treatment where two-way fixed effects do not (Goodman-Bacon, 2021).
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="capex">
  <div class="section-label">Finding 01</div>
  ## The Investment Never Moves

  If green bonds mobilised new capital, treated firms should pull away from their matched controls after issuance. The Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) event study, run over a five-year window each side, shows they do not. Pre-issuance the two groups move together, aside from a single isolated point that the joint parallel-trends test treats as noise (p = 0.63).

  After issuance every yearly effect sits on the zero line with intervals that cross it. The pooled effect is a tight null, an ATT of about 0.002 on capital expenditure scaled by assets, economically trivial and statistically indistinguishable from zero. Investment does not respond.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="robustness">
  <div class="section-label">Finding 02</div>
  ## The Null Survives Everything

  A sceptic's first move is to attack the comparison group. Swapping the strict conventional control pool for the broad Compustat universe barely moves the estimate (0.003 versus 0.004) and it stays insignificant, so the null is not an artefact of a narrow counterfactual. The divergence from Fatica and Panzica (2021) is one of sample and estimator, not control choice.

  Every specification tells the same story. The CAPEX effect holds near zero across coarsening choices (four to eight bins) and the post-2015 cohorts, and each interval crosses zero. A placebo two years before issuance and a high-disclosure subset return the same null on carbon intensity.
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="verdict">
  <div class="section-label">Synthesis</div>
  ## The Mechanism, Measured

  Put back together, the assumed machine has no working links. The investment effect is a tight null. The carbon-intensity effect is the weakest of the three: its interval is so wide it spans large reductions and increases, so it is uninformative rather than a clean zero.

  The certification split is the same. With 121 externally reviewed and 58 self-labelled treated firms, the reviewed-minus-self-labelled gap is the size of its own standard error. None of the three links is detectable. The result aligns with Mao (2024) and Lam and Wurgler (2024).
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="takeaway">
  <div class="section-label">The Takeaway</div>
  ## Identifier, Not Engine

  Why nothing moves comes down to capital fungibility. A green bond is backed by the issuer's whole cash flow, not ring-fenced to new projects, and there is no enforceable green default. A firm can point proceeds at a project it would have built anyway, fund the rest of its business with the capital that frees up, and leave total investment unchanged. The label of the financing moves; the level does not (Mao, 2024; Gyura, 2020).

  So the arrow runs the other way. Issuance does not make a firm greener; firms that are already green issue to reveal it (Spence, 1978; Riley, 1979). That makes a green bond a useful identification instrument, for screening, transition-risk pricing and portfolio allocation, rather than a lever on behaviour. It is not greenwashing: issuers that label eligible projects and report allocation follow the rules, and any gap between label and behaviour is a property of the instrument's design (Lyon and Montgomery, 2015).
